§ 01 ABOUT THE PROJECT

About the Project

On 4 March 2022, just eight days after the full-scale Russian invasion of Ukraine, the Russian State Duma adopted Federal Laws No. 31-FZ and No. 32-FZ in a single day — one of the fastest legislative procedures in modern Russian history. The laws introduced criminal liability for the dissemination of "false information" about the Russian Armed Forces and for "discrediting" them, with penalties of up to 15 years imprisonment. In practice, calling the war a "war" instead of a "special military operation" became a criminal offence.

The immediate consequences were dramatic. Independent media outlets — Novaya Gazeta, TV Rain, Meduza — suspended operations or relocated abroad. Over 150 journalists left Russia within days of the law being signed. Roskomnadzor, the federal media regulator, began blocking websites and issuing warnings at an unprecedented scale. OVD-Info, the human rights monitoring project, documented over 10,000 prosecutions under the new laws in the following years.

The human cost was equally severe. Alexei Gorinov, a Moscow municipal deputy, became the first person imprisoned under Article 207.3 — sentenced to seven years for calling the invasion a "war" at a council meeting. Vladimir Kara-Murza was sentenced to 25 years, the harshest sentence handed to a political opponent since the start of the war. Marina Ovsyannikova interrupted a live state television broadcast with an anti-war sign and was subsequently sentenced to 8.5 years in absentia after fleeing to France.

The international response came gradually. Amnesty International condemned the laws within ten days of their adoption — publishing the report End Censorship on Voices Against the War. The Bucha massacre, which emerged in April 2022, triggered a new wave of prosecutions against those who reported on it. Only in February 2025 did the European Court of Human Rights issue a formal ruling — the ECHR ruling — finding the laws in violation of the European Convention on Human Rights.

This project documents the legal, political, and human consequences of the Russian Censorship Policy 2022 and the Russo-Ukrainian war through a Linked Open Data approach, connecting eleven key entities to international authority files and formal ontologies.

§ 02 SELECTED ITEMS

Selected Items

The following are the 11 selected items chosen for the domain of study on Russian censorship policy enacted in 2022.

01

Russo-Ukrainian war

The armed conflict between Russia and Ukraine that began in 2014 and escalated into a full-scale invasion on 24 February 2022, directly triggering the adoption of Russia's war censorship laws.
02

Federal Law No. 32-FZ of 4 March 2022

Federal law introducing criminal liability for disseminating "false information" about the Russian Armed Forces, with penalties of up to 15 years imprisonment; the primary legal instrument of Russia's war censorship regime.
03

Novaya Gazeta

Independent Russian newspaper forced to suspend publication in March 2022 after receiving warnings from Roskomnadzor under the new censorship laws; its media licence was revoked in September 2022.
04

Marina Ovsyannikova

Russian journalist and former Channel One editor who interrupted a live state television broadcast on 14 March 2022 holding an anti-war sign, becoming an international symbol of dissent; subsequently sentenced to 8.5 years in absentia in October 2023.
05

Amnesty International Report

Official statement by Amnesty International condemning the Russian authorities' crackdown on independent media and anti-war protesters, urging repeal of the laws adopted on 4 March 2022.
06

Bucha massacre

The mass killing of Ukrainian civilians by Russian forces during the occupation of Bucha, evidence of which emerged on 1 April 2022 — a trigger for criminal cases under the fakes law against those who reported on the atrocities.
07

OVD-Info

Independent Russian human rights media project monitoring political persecution, which documented over 10,000 prosecutions under the 2022 war censorship laws and provided legal assistance to those charged.
08

Roskomnadzor

Russia's federal media regulator responsible for enforcing the 2022 censorship laws, including blocking news websites, issuing warnings to media outlets, and overseeing the mass exodus of independent Russian media.
09

ECHR ruling

Judgment by the European Court of Human Rights finding that Russia's war censorship laws violated the European Convention on Human Rights, marking the first international legal ruling against the legislation.
10

Alexei Gorinov

Moscow municipal deputy who became the first person sentenced to imprisonment under Article 207.3 of the Criminal Code, receiving a seven-year prison term in July 2022 for calling Russia's invasion of Ukraine a "war" at a council meeting.
11

Vladimir Kara-Murza

Russian-British opposition politician sentenced to 25 years in prison in April 2023 for his speech to the Arizona House of Representatives criticising the invasion — the harshest sentence handed to a political opponent since the start of the war.
§ 03 KNOWLEDGE ORGANIZATION

Conceptual Map & E/R Model

The main topic of this project is the Russian Censorship Policy 2022. The eleven items selected are all relevant in order to document the legal, political, and human consequences of the war censorship laws enacted on 4 March 2022. They include the armed conflict that triggered the legislation, the law itself, the government body responsible for its enforcement, independent media organisations silenced by it, individuals prosecuted under it, a human rights report condemning it, a key event that escalated its application, a monitoring organisation that documented its effects, and the international legal ruling that found it in violation of human rights law.

The Conceptual Map represents the extracted data in natural language — the relationships as found across the items and their attributes. The central node represents the main topic, coloured nodes represent the eleven items, and plain nodes represent their attributes and values.

The E/R Model translates the Conceptual Map into a more formal and abstract representation. Each concrete instance is replaced with its general class — Person, Organization, Document, Event, Policy, Place, Date, Concept, Literal — and the relationships between them are expressed as abstract predicates. This abstraction allows the domain to be represented from a more general perspective, independent of the specific items chosen.

§ 05 METADATA ALIGNMENT

Metadata Alignment

The alignment table maps each property identified in the domain to its equivalent term across all standards used in the RDF serialisation, organised by WHO, WHAT, WHEN, and WHERE. Dublin Core provides a universal baseline and MARC21 covers library catalogue records. Schema.org captures attribute-level properties of persons and organisations — such as schema:jobTitle and schema:foundingDate — while CIDOC-CRM expresses complex historical relationships with sufficient precision, such as causation (crm:P17_was_motivated_by), movement (crm:P26_moved_to), and enforcement (crm:P14_carried_out_by). BIBO provides dedicated classes for bibliographic and legal documents — bibo:Report and bibo:LegalDocument — where Dublin Core's generic description is insufficient. Agreements Ontology covers the federal law through aio:Law, modelling it as a binding social contract rather than a generic document. Where no suitable term exists in a given standard, N/A is indicated.

§ 06 THEORETICAL MODEL

Theoretical Model & Enhanced E/R Model

The Theoretical Model provides a detailed list of properties for each item in the dataset, enriching the Conceptual Map with additional information retrieved from authoritative external sources including Wikidata, VIAF, the Library of Congress, and official institutional websites. § 08 CONCEPTUAL MODEL

Conceptual Model

Transitioning from the Theoretical Model to the Conceptual Model, the most suitable ontologies were identified to represent the project's items in detail. As a starting point, the Linked Open Vocabularies (LOV) search engine was used to identify the most appropriate and widely adopted vocabularies. The most common ontologies were preferred to ensure data interoperability.

§ 09 ITEMS DESCRIPTION

Items Description

This section presents the formal description of each item in the dataset as a set of RDF triples. Each entity is identified by a URI in camelCase notation and described through subject-predicate-object statements using terms drawn from the adopted ontologies: CIDOC-CRM, Dublin Core, Schema.org, BIBO, and Agreements Ontology. URIs refer to other entities within the dataset or to external authority records, while literal values represent strings, dates, and descriptive information. Temporal values are represented as intermediate nodes of type crm:E52_Time-Span, following the CIDOC-CRM convention for time spans.

TEI → RDF Transformation Pipeline

This section documents the transformation pipeline used to convert TEI/XML into RDF triples. The process is implemented through Python scripts that extract semantic annotations from the TEI structure and map them to RDF classes and properties aligned with the selected ontologies.

The workflow includes the TEI/XML source file, an XSLT stylesheet for XML-to-HTML transformation, generated HTML output, and Python scripts used for XML-to-HTML and XML-to-RDF conversion processes.
