Russian 2022 War Censorship Laws

On Amendments to the Criminal Code of the Russian Federation and Articles 31 and 151 of the Criminal Procedure Code of the Russian Federation are a group of federal laws promulgated by the Russian government during the Russo-Ukrainian war. These laws establish administrative and criminal punishments for "discrediting" or dissemination of "unreliable information" about the Russian Armed Forces, other Russian state bodies and their operations, and for calls to impose sanctions against Russia, Russian organizations and citizens. These laws are sometimes referred to as the fakes laws.

The laws have been strongly condemned by the political opposition and by human rights groups. The adoption of these laws resulted in the mass exodus of foreign media from Russia and the termination of war reporting by independent Russian media. More than 10,000 people have been prosecuted under these laws, according to human rights NGO OVD-Info.

Federal Laws No. 31-FZ and No. 32-FZ

On 4 March 2022, Russian Federal Laws No. 31-FZ and No. 32-FZ were adopted by the State Duma, approved by the Federation Council and signed by President Vladimir Putin. The latter supplemented the Criminal Code of the Russian Federation with articles 207.3, 280.3 and 284.2. The dissemination of "unreliable information" about the armed forces and its operation could be punished with up to fifteen years imprisonment. These laws are enforced by Roskomnadzor, Russia's federal media regulator.

Impact on Independent Media

Many Russian media outlets were forced to stop covering the Russian invasion of Ukraine because of this legislation. Independent newspaper Novaya Gazeta was forced to suspend publication in March 2022 after receiving warnings from Roskomnadzor under the new censorship laws. Its media licence was subsequently revoked in September 2022. According to news website Agentstvo, over 150 journalists had left Russia by 7 March 2022, within three days of the laws coming into effect.

Notable Prosecutions

Alexei Gorinov

The first person imprisoned under criminal code article 207.3 is Alexei Gorinov, a then-60-year-old deputy at Moscow's Krasnoselsky district council, engineer, lawyer and human rights defender. At a council meeting on 15 March 2022 he said: "How can we talk about a children's drawing competition, when children are dying every day?!" Gorinov was convicted on 8 July 2022 to seven years imprisonment. On 29 November 2024, Gorinov was sentenced to three additional years in prison, on charges of "advocating terrorism".

Marina Ovsyannikova

On 4 October 2023, Russian journalist Marina Ovsyannikova, former Channel One editor, was sentenced in absentia to 8.5 years in prison for "spreading false information" about the Russian Army. Ovsyannikova had interrupted a live state television broadcast on 14 March 2022 holding an anti-war sign, becoming an international symbol of dissent. She subsequently fled to Paris, France.

Vladimir Kara-Murza

On 22 April 2022, Russian opposition politician Vladimir Kara-Murza was charged by a Russian court for spreading of false information about the Russian military, due to his 15 March speech to the Arizona House of Representatives, in which he denounced the war in Ukraine. Kara-Murza was sentenced to 25 years in prison in April 2023, the harshest sentence handed to a political opponent since the start of the war. He was released in August 2024 as part of a prisoner exchange.

Bucha Massacre and Escalation

The laws were applied extensively following the revelation of the Bucha massacre in April 2022. Criminal cases were initiated against bloggers and journalists who reported on the deaths of civilian population in Bucha, Ukraine. Russian journalist Ilya Krasilshchik was charged by a Russian court for spreading fake news about the massacre in the Ukrainian city of Bucha.

International Response

Amnesty International

On 14 March 2022, Amnesty International published a statement — "End Censorship on Voices Against the War" — criticizing Russian laws promulgated on 4 March 2022. Amnesty International strongly condemned the escalating attack against civil society organizations and independent media unleashed by the Russian authorities since the start of Russia's invasion of Ukraine, and urged the Russian authorities to abide by their international human rights obligations to respect the rights to freedom of expression, association and peaceful assembly.

European Court of Human Rights

On 11 February 2025, the European Court of Human Rights issued a ruling finding that Russia's 2022 wartime censorship laws were illegal and violated the right to freedom of expression.

OVD-Info Monitoring

According to human rights NGO OVD-Info, as of August 2024, Russian authorities opened more than 10,000 cases against people accused of "discrediting" the Russian armed forces. OVD-Info has monitored political persecution under the laws and provided legal assistance to those charged throughout the period.
